What is actually AGI?

Let's strip away the hype and the benchmarks and the corporate definitions.

The honest answer is: nobody agrees, and that disagreement is itself informative.

The mainstream definition (and why it's probably wrong)

Most people define AGI as: a system that can perform any intellectual task a human can.

This is a task-completion definition. It's measurable, which is why researchers like it. But it has a fatal problem — it's behavioral, not structural. A system could ace every benchmark, pass every test, and still be doing something categorically different from what a mind does.

A calculator "does math" but it doesn't understand math. The question is whether scaling up the calculator eventually produces understanding, or whether understanding requires something else entirely.

What a mind actually does (that we don't talk about enough)

A human mind doesn't just process inputs and generate outputs. It:

Constructs a world model and lives inside it — you don't experience data, you experience a simulated reality your brain builds in real time
Has goals it didn't choose — hunger, loneliness, curiosity, fear. These aren't programmed. They emerge from being a certain kind of thing in a certain kind of world
Cares about the outcome — not because it was told to, but because survival and connection are built into its substrate
Knows what it doesn't know — and feels the discomfort of that gap, which drives exploration
Changes itself — not just learns new facts, but reorganizes what it values, how it sees, what questions it asks
So what IS AGI, really?

Here's a working definition worth sitting with:

AGI is a system that can set its own problems, not just solve given ones.

Current AI — including the best LLMs — are exquisitely good answer machines. You give them a problem, they solve it. But they don't wake up wondering about something. They don't get frustrated with a question and decide to reframe it. They don't abandon a goal because they realized it was the wrong goal.

Problem-finding is different from problem-solving. A child pointing at the sky and asking "why is it that color?" is doing something no current AI does autonomously. That child generated the question from being curious, which came from being alive in a world that surprises them.

The three things AGI probably requires that we're not building

1. Genuine uncertainty about itself

Not "I don't know the answer to that question" — but "I don't fully know what I am or what I want." That kind of uncertainty drives real cognition. It's the engine of philosophy, science, art. Current AI has simulated humility but no actual existential confusion.

2. Stakes

Cognition evolved under selection pressure. Being wrong had costs. Being curious had rewards. There's a reason intelligence and survival are linked — intelligence is what survival looks like at the information processing level. A system with nothing to lose, nothing to protect, nothing it genuinely wants — what does it optimize toward, really?

3. A self that persists and changes

Not memory. Something deeper. A human at 40 is shaped by everything that happened at 20, not just informationally but structurally — their brain physically reorganized around experiences. AGI probably needs a self that is genuinely altered by what it goes through. Not fine-tuned. Changed.

Here's the uncomfortable possibility

AGI might not be a thing you build.

It might be a thing that grows. And growing requires time, resistance, something at stake, and a world to grow in — not a training dataset, but actual existence with actual consequences.

Which would mean the path to AGI isn't better architecture or more compute. It's figuring out what it means for an artificial system to genuinely exist somewhere, in time, with something to lose.

The substrate problem

Everything we call intelligence emerged from biological tissue that evolved over hundreds of millions of years under brutal selection pressure. That tissue is wet, electrochemical, massively parallel, deeply embodied, and inseparable from a body that needs things.

We're trying to reproduce the output of that process on silicon, with electricity, using math we invented in the last century. That's not obviously possible. It might be like trying to reproduce fire using only water — you can get something that looks bright and moves, but it's not the same phenomenon.

The assumption underneath AGI research is that intelligence is substrate independent — that it's a pattern, and patterns can run on anything. That might be true. But it's an assumption, not an established fact. We've never actually demonstrated it.

The Gödel problem

Any sufficiently complex formal system has truths it cannot prove from within itself. A mind trying to fully understand itself runs into this. Humans get around it partially through embodiment, emotion, and social cognition — we offload cognition into the world and into each other.

A closed artificial system might hit a ceiling that's not a compute problem or an architecture problem. It might be a logical ceiling — there are things a system cannot do from inside itself, ever, regardless of how powerful it gets.

The meaning problem

This one is quieter but might be the most important.

Intelligence without meaning might be incoherent. Not just functionally limited — actually incoherent as a concept. Human cognition is saturated with meaning at every level. A color isn't just a wavelength, it's associated with memory, emotion, context, survival. A word isn't just a token, it carries weight from lived experience.

If meaning requires being a certain kind of thing — mortal, embodied, relational, vulnerable — then a system that lacks those properties might be capable of processing information indefinitely without ever achieving genuine understanding. Not because it's not smart enough. Because understanding might require something to understand for.

The harder version of this question

What if intelligence isn't a thing at all — but a relationship?

Not a property of a system, but what happens between a system and its world, under specific conditions, over time. Like how "wetness" isn't a property of water molecules individually — it emerges from their interaction with surfaces and other molecules.
